9. Introduction to Game Theory

9.1 Intuition and Motivation

Economics is largely about studying human behaviour, and game theory is the branch of economics and mathematics used to study what humans do when they must \textit{interact} with each other.

Note

Game theory provides a systematic way to model strategic situations: settings in which each participant’s outcome depends not only on their own choices, but also on the choices of others.

Let’s look at some everyday examples:

  • Roommates cleaning their apartment — Each person prefers a clean room, but would rather the other person do the cleaning. (This is the \textit{freeloader problem}).
  • Two people competing for the same romantic partner — Each would like to win, but is only willing to pursue if he believes that he can put in more effort than the other pusuers. (This resembles a \textit{war of attrition}).

In order to study such situations formally, we need to think about what makes up a game.

9.2 Building a Model

The first step is to recognise how many \textbf{players} (actors) are in the interaction. For now, assume there are just two players.

Since this is a ``game'' of sorts, we must specify the \textbf{actions} available to each player. Following Osborne’s notation, let $S$ denote the set of actions (or \textbf{strategies}).

Suppose each player has three actions available. This already resembles a normal human interaction: each side has multiple possible choices.

Now, to evaluate the outcomes of these actions, we need a way to \textbf{measure preferences}. In microeconomics, agents are modeled as maximizing a \textbf{utility function}. The same idea applies here: each player has payoffs that reflect how much they value different outcomes.

9.3 Example Game

Consider the following payoff matrix for a two-player game. Player 1 chooses a row, Player 2 chooses a column, and the entries represent utilities (Player 1 first, Player 2 second):

A B C
A (7,5) (0,3) (1,0)
B (5,-3) (6,2) (-2,5)
C (3,0) (2,6) (-3,1)

Note

The first number in each cell is Player 1's payoff, the second is Player 2's payoff.

9.4 Formal Definition of a Game

Definition

A (normal-form) game is defined by the triple

$$ G = (N, (S_i)_{i \in N}, (u_i)_{i \in N}) $$

where:

  • $N$ is the set of players,
  • $S_i$ is the set of strategies available to player $i$,
  • $u_i: \prod_{j \in N} S_j \to \mathbb{R}$ is the utility (payoff) function of player $i$.

Note that we are not restricting ourselves to finite sets. The strategy set of a player can be infinite. For example, a firm choosing a price may select any positive real number.

In our example, $N = \{1,2\}$, each player has three actions ($S_1 = S_2 = \{A, B, C\}$), and the utility functions $u_1, u_2$ are given by the payoff matrix above.

9.5 Solution Concepts

Having defined a game, the next natural question is:

“What will actually happen?”

If each player tries to maximize their utility, the answer is not straightforward:

  • There may be no single strategy profile that simultaneously maximizes both players’ utilities.
  • Each player only cares about their own utility, not the other’s. (Although we could model altruism, it complicates the framework and is beyond our current scope.)

The first solution concept we introduce is \textbf{domination}.

9.6 Dominated Strategies

Returning to our example: notice that for Player 1, action $C$ never makes sense. No matter what Player 2 does, Player 1 is better off choosing some other action.

Definition

A strategy $s_i \in S_i$ is \textbf{strictly dominated} if there exists another strategy $s'_i \in S_i$ such that

$$ u_i(s'_i, s_{-i}) > u_i(s_i, s_{-i}) \quad \text{for all } s_{-i} \in S_{-i}. $$

Key assumptions when modeling domination:

  1. Rational players will not choose strictly dominated strategies.
  2. All players are rational.
  3. Rationality is \textbf{common knowledge} (everyone knows, everyone knows that everyone knows, and so on).

9.7 Iterated Elimination of Strictly Dominated Strategies

If Player 2 knows Player 1 is rational, she can eliminate action $C$ of Player 1 from consideration. After this, Player 2 may realize that her own action $B$ is strictly dominated, and eliminate it as well. This process continues back and forth.

Worked Example: Iterated Elimination

  • For Player 1, $C$ is strictly dominated.
  • After $C$ is removed, for Player 2, $B$ is strictly dominated.
  • After $B$ is removed, for Player 1, $B$ is strictly dominated.
  • After this, for Player 2, $C$ is strictly dominated.

This game is solvable by iterated elimination of strictly dominated strategies, and the unique solution is:

$$(A, A)$$

Example

The \textbf{Prisoner’s Dilemma} is a famous game solvable by iterated elimination of dominated strategies.

Example: Prisoner's Dilemma

The \textbf{Prisoner’s Dilemma} is a famous game solvable by iterated elimination of dominated strategies.

Consider two prisoners deciding whether to \textit{Confess} (C) or \textit{Stay Silent} (S). The payoff matrix is:

Confess (C) Silent (S)
Confess (C) (-5,-5) (0,-10)
Silent (S) (-10,0) (-1,-1)

Here, higher numbers are better (less prison time).

Note

The dilemma arises because $(C,C)$ is the outcome predicted by iterated elimination (since confessing strictly dominates staying silent), but this outcome is \textbf{inefficient}: both players would prefer $(S,S)$, which gives them only $-1$ years each instead of $-5$.

9.8 Weak Dominance

So far, we have considered only \textbf{strict domination}, where one strategy is always strictly better than another. But sometimes a strategy can be at least as good in all cases, and strictly better in some cases. This leads us to \textbf{weak dominance}.

L M R
U (2,-8) (0,0) (3,1)
D (1,-8) (0,5) (3,0)

Here, notice:

  • For Player 1, strategy $D$ is \textbf{weakly dominated} by $U$: $U$ yields a higher payoff when Player 2 plays $L$, and the same payoff when Player 2 plays $M$ or $R$.
  • For Player 2, strategy $L$ is \textbf{strictly dominated} by $M$ and $R$.

Definition

A strategy $s_i \in S_i$ is \textbf{weakly dominated} if there exists another strategy $s'_i \in S_i$ such that

$$ u_i(s'_i, s_{-i}) \geq u_i(s_i, s_{-i}) \quad \text{for all } s_{-i} \in S_{-i}, $$

and

$$ u_i(s'_i, s_{-i}) > u_i(s_i, s_{-i}) \quad \text{for at least one } s_{-i} \in S_{-i}. $$

Worked Example: Weak Dominance

  1. $D$ is weakly dominated by $U$ for Player 1.
  2. $L$ is strictly dominated for Player 2 (by $M$ and $R$).
  3. If we eliminate $D$ first (for Player 1), then both $L$ and $M$ are dominated for Player 2, leaving $(U,R)$.
  4. If we eliminate $L$ first (for Player 2), then we are left with all profiles in $\{U,D\} \times \{M,R\}$.

Note

Our fourth assumption is that rational players do \textbf{not} play weakly dominated strategies.

10. Nash Equilibrium and Best Responses

10.1 Example (no dominated strategies)

Consider the following payoff matrix (Player 1 = rows, Player 2 = columns):

L C R
T (0,6) (6,0) (4,3)
M (6,0) (0,6) (4,3)
B (3,3) (3,3) (5,5)

There are no strictly dominated strategies in this game.

10.2 Best Response

Definition: Best Response

Given a game $G=(N,(S_i),(u_i))$, the \textbf{best response} of player $i$ to a profile of the other players' strategies $s_{-i}\in S_{-i}$ is any strategy $s_i\in S_i$ that maximizes $i$'s payoff holding $s_{-i}$ fixed:

$$ \mathrm{BR}_i(s_{-i}) \;=\; \arg\max_{s_i\in S_i} u_i(s_i, s_{-i}). $$

Definition: Best-Response Correspondence

The \textbf{best-response correspondence} (or mapping) for player $i$ is the set-valued function $\mathrm{BR}_i: S_{-i}\rightrightarrows S_i$ that assigns to each $s_{-i}$ the set $\mathrm{BR}_i(s_{-i})$ of all best responses.

10.3 Worked best-response calculations (the example above)

Compute best replies for each player's possible opponent actions:

  • If Player 2 plays \textbf{L}: Player 1's payoffs are $T:0, M:6, B:3$ so $\mathrm{BR}_1(L)=\{M\}$.
  • If Player 2 plays \textbf{C}: Player 1's payoffs are $T:6, M:0, B:3$ so $\mathrm{BR}_1(C)=\{T\}$.
  • If Player 2 plays \textbf{R}: Player 1's payoffs are $T:4, M:4, B:5$ so $\mathrm{BR}_1(R)=\{B\}$.
  • If Player 1 plays \textbf{T}: Player 2's payoffs are $L:6, C:0, R:3$ so $\mathrm{BR}_2(T)=\{L\}$.
  • If Player 1 plays \textbf{M}: Player 2's payoffs are $L:0, C:6, R:3$ so $\mathrm{BR}_2(M)=\{C\}$.
  • If Player 1 plays \textbf{B}: Player 2's payoffs are $L:3, C:3, R:5$ so $\mathrm{BR}_2(B)=\{R\}$.

Reading off the mutual best replies we find the candidate pairs:

$$(M,L),\ (T,C),\ (B,R).$$

Checking mutuality we see only

$$(B,R)$$

is mutual: $\mathrm{BR}_1(R)=\{B\}$ and $\mathrm{BR}_2(B)=\{R\}$. Thus $(B,R)$ is a \textbf{pure-strategy Nash equilibrium} and is \emph{stable} in the sense that neither player has a profitable unilateral deviation when the profile is $(B,R)$.

The other pairs form a best-response \emph{cycle}:

$$T \xrightarrow{\mathrm{BR}_2} L \xrightarrow{\mathrm{BR}_1} M \xrightarrow{\mathrm{BR}_2} C \xrightarrow{\mathrm{BR}_1} T \cdots$$

so they are not self-enforcing.

Observation

The pair $(B,R)$ is the unique pure Nash equilibrium. It is the only profile where both players are simultaneously playing best responses to each other, hence no profitable unilateral deviation exists.

10.4 Formal Definition of Nash Equilibrium

Definition: Pure-Strategy Nash Equilibrium

A strategy profile $s^\ast=(s^\ast_i)_{i\in N}\in \prod_{i\in N} S_i$ is a \textbf{(pure-strategy) Nash equilibrium} if for every player $i\in N$,

$$ u_i(s^\ast_i, s^\ast_{-i}) \ge u_i(s_i, s^\ast_{-i}) \quad \text{for all } s_i\in S_i. $$

Equivalently,

$$ s^\ast_i \in \mathrm{BR}_i(s^\ast_{-i}) \quad\text{for every } i. $$

Intuition: at a Nash equilibrium no single player can gain by unilaterally changing her strategy.

10.5 Coordination Game (pure-strategy solutions)

A simple pure coordination game (2×2) — players get payoff $1$ if they coordinate on the same action, $0$ otherwise:

L R
L (1,1) (0,0)
R (0,0) (1,1)
  • Pure Nash equilibria: $(L,L)$ and $(R,R)$.
  • Both are mutual best responses and thus stable; which one is played may depend on pre-play communication, focal points, or history.

10.6 Bach & Stravinsky (Battle of the Sexes) — pure-strategy solutions

This is a coordination-with-conflict example. Two players want to go out together but prefer different events. Payoffs often given as:

Bach Stravinsky
Bach (2,1) (0,0)
Stravinsky (0,0) (1,2)

Interpretation: Player 1 prefers Bach (gets 2 when both choose Bach), Player 2 prefers Stravinsky (gets 2 when both choose Stravinsky), but both prefer coordinating to not coordinating.

  • Pure Nash equilibria: $(\text{Bach},\text{Bach})$ and $(\text{Stravinsky},\text{Stravinsky})$.
  • There is also a mixed-strategy equilibrium (see below), but without mixing the two pure equilibria are the obvious stable outcomes.

10.7 Remark on Pure Equilibria

Pure Nash equilibria capture the idea of mutual best responses. Some games (like Matching Pennies) have no pure equilibria and require mixed strategies; others (like coordination or Battle of the Sexes) have multiple pure equilibria and selection arguments are needed to predict which will occur.

10.8 Mixed Strategies: Introduction

Up to now, we have assumed players always choose a single action. Such a choice is called a \textbf{pure strategy}.

But what if a player randomizes between their available actions? This leads us to the idea of \textbf{mixed strategies}.

Definition: Mixed Strategy

A mixed strategy for player $i$ is a probability distribution $\sigma_i$ over the set $S_i$ of pure strategies.

We denote mixed strategies by $\sigma_i$ and pure strategies by $s_i$.

Note

Intuitively, playing a mixed strategy is like being “schizophrenic” — instead of sticking with one choice, you randomize between them.

11. Mixed Strategies

11.1 Matching Pennies

Example: Matching Pennies

Two players simultaneously choose Head (H) or Tail (T). If the coins match Player 1 wins $1 (Player 2 loses $1); if they differ Player 2 wins $1 (Player 1 loses $1). The payoff matrix (Player 1 first, Player 2 second) is:

H T
H (1,-1) (-1,1)
T (-1,1) (1,-1)

There is no pure-strategy Nash equilibrium: any pure strategy is exploited by the opponent and leads to a best-response cycle.

11.2 The Set of Mixed Strategies

Definition: Set of Mixed Strategies

For a player $i$ with finite pure strategy set $S_i=\{s_1,\dots,s_k\}$, the \textbf{set of mixed strategies} for player $i$ is

$$ \Sigma_i \;=\; \Big\{ \sigma_i: S_i\to[0,1]\ \Big|\ \sum_{s\in S_i}\sigma_i(s)=1\Big\}. $$

We think of $\sigma_i(s)$ as the probability that player $i$ plays pure strategy $s$.

Properties of $\sigma_i$:

  • $\sigma_i(s)\ge 0$ for each $s\in S_i$,
  • $\sum_{s\in S_i}\sigma_i(s)=1$,
  • when $|S_i|=k$ the set $\Sigma_i$ is the $(k-1)$-dimensional simplex; if $k=2$ we can describe $\sigma_i$ by a single number $p=\sigma_i(s_1)$ because $\sigma_i(s_2)=1-p$.

Simplex picture (2 actions)

For $S_i=\{H,T\}$ we can identify a mixed strategy by $p=\sigma_i(H)\in[0,1]$. The simplex $\Sigma_i$ is the line segment

$\sigma_i(H)+\sigma_i(T)=1,\qquad \sigma_i(H),\sigma_i(T)\ge0.$

11.3 Mixed Strategy Profiles and Expected Utility

Definition: Mixed Strategy Profile

A \textbf{mixed strategy profile} is $\sigma=(\sigma_i)_{i\in N}\in \prod_{i\in N}\Sigma_i$. Denote others' profile by $\sigma_{-i}$.

Definition: Expected Utility

The \textbf{expected utility} for player $i$ under profile $\sigma$ is

$$ U_i(\sigma) \;=\; \sum_{s\in \prod_j S_j} \Big(\prod_{j\in N}\sigma_j(s_j)\Big)\, u_i(s), $$

where $s=(s_j)_{j\in N}$ is a pure action profile and $u_i(s)$ is the payoff when pure profile $s$ occurs.

Note

We assume independence: the mixed strategies of different players are statistically independent, so the probability of pure profile $s$ is $\prod_j\sigma_j(s_j)$.

11.4 Nash Equilibrium with Mixed Strategies

Definition: Mixed-Strategy Nash Equilibrium

A mixed strategy profile $\sigma^\ast$ is a \textbf{(mixed-strategy) Nash equilibrium} if for every player $i$,

$$ U_i(\sigma^\ast_i,\sigma^\ast_{-i}) \;\ge\; U_i(\sigma_i,\sigma^\ast_{-i}) \quad\text{for all }\sigma_i\in\Sigma_i. $$

Equivalently, each player's $\sigma^\ast_i$ is a best response (maximizer of expected utility) to $\sigma^\ast_{-i}$.

Characterization due to multi-linearity.

A mixed strategy profile $\sigma^\ast$ is a \textbf{(mixed-strategy) Nash equilibrium} if and only if for every player $i$,

$$ U_i(\sigma^\ast_i,\sigma^\ast_{-i}) \;\ge\; U_i(s_i,\sigma^\ast_{-i}) \quad\text{for all }s_i\in S_i. $$

Equivalently, each player's $\sigma^\ast_i$ is a best response (maximizer of expected utility) to $\sigma^\ast_{-i}$.

Nash Existence Theorem (finite games).

Every finite game has at least one Nash equilibrium in mixed strategies. (Nash, 1950.)

11.5 Battle of the Sexes (Bach & Stravinsky) — two computations of the mixed equilibrium

Recall the pure-payoff matrix:

Bach (B) Stravinsky (S)
Bach (B) (2,1) (0,0)
Stravinsky (S) (0,0) (1,2)

Denote $\sigma_1(B)=x$ and $\sigma_2(B)=y$ (so Player 1 plays Bach with probability $x$, Player 2 with probability $y$).

Method 1: Best-response correspondence

Compute expected payoffs (Player 1):

$$ U_1(B\mid y)=2y + 0(1-y)=2y,\qquad U_1(S\mid y)=0\cdot y + 1(1-y)=1-y. $$

Thus Player 1's best response correspondence is

$$ \mathrm{BR}_1(y)= \begin{cases} \{B\} & \text{if } y>\tfrac{1}{3},\\[4pt] \{S\} & \text{if } y<\tfrac{1}{3},\\[4pt] \text{any }x\in[0,1] & \text{if } y=\tfrac{1}{3}. \end{cases} $$

For Player 2:

$$ U_2(B\mid x)=1\cdot x + 0(1-x)=x,\qquad U_2(S\mid x)=0\cdot x + 2(1-x)=2-2x, $$

so

$$ \mathrm{BR}_2(x)= \begin{cases} \{B\} & \text{if } x>\tfrac{2}{3},\\[4pt] \{S\} & \text{if } x<\tfrac{2}{3},\\[4pt] \text{any }y\in[0,1] & \text{if } x=\tfrac{2}{3}. \end{cases} $$

Graphically (in the unit square for $(x,y)$) each BR correspondence is a step function; intersections of the two correspondences give Nash equilibria. The intersections are:

  • Pure equilibria: $(x,y)=(1,1)$ i.e. (B,B) and $(0,0)$ i.e. (S,S).
  • An interior mixed equilibrium at $(x,y)=(\tfrac{2}{3},\tfrac{1}{3})$ where the two step functions meet (Player 1 is indifferent when $y=1/3$, Player 2 is indifferent when $x=2/3$).

Method 2: Indifference principle

Make each player's opponent indifferent between their pure strategies.

Player 1 indifferent: $U_1(B\mid y)=U_1(S\mid y)$ gives

$$ 2y = 1-y \quad\Rightarrow\quad y=\tfrac{1}{3}. $$

Player 2 indifferent: $U_2(B\mid x)=U_2(S\mid x)$ gives

$$ x = 2-2x \quad\Rightarrow\quad x=\tfrac{2}{3}. $$

Thus the mixed Nash equilibrium is $(x,y)=(\tfrac{2}{3},\tfrac{1}{3})$.

11.6 Worked 2×2 example (indifference calculation)

Consider the game

L R
T (1,-1) (0,2)
B (0,1) (2,0)

Let $x$ be the probability Player 1 plays $T$ and $y$ the probability Player 2 plays $L$.

Compute expected payoffs for Player 1:

$$ U_1(T\mid y)=y, \qquad U_1(B\mid y)=2-2y. $$

Indifference for Player 1: $y=2-2y \Rightarrow 3y=2 \Rightarrow y=\tfrac{2}{3}$.

Compute expected payoffs for Player 2:

$$ U_2(L\mid x)=1-2x, \qquad U_2(R\mid x)=2x. $$

Indifference for Player 2: $1-2x = 2x \Rightarrow 1 = 4x \Rightarrow x=\tfrac{1}{4}$.

Hence the unique (interior) mixed Nash equilibrium is

$$\sigma_1=(x=\tfrac{1}{4}),\qquad \sigma_2=(y=\tfrac{2}{3})$$

11.7 Miscellaneous remarks

  • The indifference principle is powerful for 2×2 (and small) games: to compute a mixed equilibrium find probabilities that make opponents indifferent between the pure strategies they mix over.
  • A mixed strategy can sometimes (weakly or strictly) dominate a pure strategy: when the expected payoff from randomization exceeds that of a pure action for every opponent profile.
  • Matching Pennies illustrates why mixed strategies are essential: the only equilibrium is mixed (each player randomizes with $p=\tfrac{1}{2}$).

11.8 Dominance and Mixed Strategies

Consider the following game:

L R
T (3,0) (0,1)
M (0,0) (3,1)
B (1,1) (1,0)

There is no pure strategy dominance, but consider mixed strategies.

Suppose Row plays the mixed strategy

$$ \sigma_R(T)=\tfrac{1}{2},\quad \sigma_R(M)=\tfrac{1}{2},\quad \sigma_R(B)=0, $$

and Column plays

$$ \sigma_C(L)=y,\quad \sigma_C(R)=1-y. $$

Then Row’s expected payoff is

$$ U_R(\sigma_R,\sigma_C) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot y\cdot 3 \;+\; \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot (1-y)\cdot 3  = \tfrac{3}{2}(y+1-y)=\tfrac{3}{2}, \quad \forall y\in[0,1]. $$

If instead Row plays $B$, then

$$ U_R(B,\sigma_C) = 1\cdot y + 1\cdot (1-y) = 1, \quad \forall y\in[0,1]. $$

Thus $B$ is \textbf{strictly dominated} by the mixed strategy $\sigma_R=(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},0)$.

  • Eliminating $B$ makes $L$ dominated by $R$ for Column.
  • Eliminating $L$ then makes $T$ dominated by $M$ for Row.

Conclusion on Iterated Elimination

The only profile that survives iterated elimination is $(M,R)$.
